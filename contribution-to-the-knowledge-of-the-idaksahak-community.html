Contribution to the Knowledge of the Idaksahak Community

By Ahmed Mohamed Ag Guidi

Retired Geological Engineer, former official at the Ministry of Mines, Energy, and Water of Mali, and a member of the Idaksahak community.

Introduction

This note, while not an in-depth study of the Idaksahak community, aims to introduce its main characteristics. It draws from oral traditions, testimonies gathered from elders, certain written works, as well as the personal experience of the author.

Etymology of “Idaksahak”

The name Idaksahak, which the community uses for itself, is believed to derive from a contraction of the term "Id‑Agg Ishak". Neighboring communities refer to them as Daw‑Sahak or Daw‑Ishak, depending on how they pronounce Ishak, a Songhai name rather than the Arabic Ishaq of the Quran or Isaac of the Bible.

It may thus refer to a king’s name, specifically Ishak I, a Songhai emperor of the Middle Ages.

Naturally, the prefix Id‑Agg (“son of” in Tamasheq) followed by the name of a dominant leader was common in the Sudan region—particularly around Timbuktu before colonization—used to designate a community without implying a genealogical lineage.

The same applies with the term Daw or Idaw or Oulad (“son of” in Arab-Berber), followed by the name of a respected leader, scholar, or spiritual figure. In all cases, a prefix plus name becomes the label for an entire community.

In this light, Dawsahak hardly suggests a genealogical descent from Ishaq or Isaac of Jewish lineage.

Nonetheless, there is a tendency to associate the name with the Quranic Is‑haq or the Biblical Isaac, implying a possible Israelite or Jewish origin. This cannot be ruled out in the absence of concrete evidence—but it is verifiable today through DNA analysis.

Geographical and Historical Origin

The Idaksahak recall migrating from Tafilalet (southern Morocco) via Timbuktu. They assert they were among the first groups to settle in the Ménaka region, well before the arrival of Tuareg tribes such as Kel Dinnik, Imghad, and Kel Tamajak.

They helped build the administrative infrastructure of Ménaka during colonial times and took part in defending the territory by forming patrols against raiding parties.

Oral memory recalls an Idaksahak leader, Abdoulaye Ould Kati, who supported the French in the capture of Gao. He was later appointed in charge of both the Kel Essouk and the Idaksahak.

After Mali’s independence, many Idaksahak joined state institutions—security forces, education, and administration—while others continued their traditional livestock activities. The community maintained strong ties with Algeria, Niger, and Libya, where much of their diaspora lives.

Seasonal cross-border movements are vital for sustaining their pastoral economy. The Idaksahak demonstrate remarkable mobility, adaptability, and extensive social and commercial networks.

Language, Culture, and Religion

The Idaksahak speak Tadaksahak, a mixed language with a Songhai base enriched with Tamasheq, Arabic, and even French loanwords. It is primarily spoken in the Ménaka and Talatayt regions and is a strong marker of Idaksahak identity.

Tadaksahak also serves as a liturgical and scholarly language. Religious texts are often memorized and passed down in this language. Additionally, they are also proficient in Tamasheq and Songhai, facilitating communication with neighboring communities.

Religiously, the Idaksahak are known for their devout practice of Sunni Islam, following the Malikite rite. Most religious leaders (marabouts, imams, preachers) come from their community. Their religious commitment is associated with strong moral discipline, and several families are renowned for their Quranic knowledge.

“The tandem of religious knowledge and economic power has always been a source of pride and independence for the Idaksahak compared to neighboring communities.”

This religious importance is reflected in educational choices (preference for Quranic schools), social organization (respect for scholars), and traditional rites (marriages, funerals, religious festivals) that follow Islamic traditions.

Contemporary History and Political Assertion

Long perceived as a peaceful community, the Idaksahak began shifting their stance in the 1990s by engaging in Tuareg rebellions. Their involvement was driven by needs to protect their property, defend their interests, and gain political voice.

In 2016, the establishment of the Movement for the Salvation of Azawad (MSA) by Moussa Ag Acharatouman marked a significant turning point. For the first time, a politico-military movement explicitly aimed to defend the rights and interests of the Idaksahak.

This political emergence was not without its internal tensions. Some traditional leaders viewed it as a threat to their authority. Additionally, part of the marginalized Idaksahak youth was attracted to jihadist groups such as MUJAO or EIGS, posing serious dilemmas for the community.

“Members of a tribe, by virtue of blood ties and their shared history, would mobilize as one in a conflict… This assumption deserves nuance.”

Recent conflicts have highlighted diverse political paths and viewpoints within the Idaksahak. This complexity invites a reassessment of notions like tribal solidarity and political unity, which are often idealized.
